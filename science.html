The Science behind Color Theory

To start to understand color theory, we need to understand how we can even see colors in the first place.

We are able to see colours because of the interactions between our brain, eyes, and light. When light hits an object, it is either reflected or absorbed. The reflected light enters our eyes and into the retina, where millions of light receptors known as rods and cones detect different wavelengths of light.
Most people have three kinds of cone cells which are sensitive to different wavelengths. These are known as the blue, green, red cones. Which means that colorblind people may have trouble discerning colours because of a deficiency/absence in one (or more) type of cone.

These cones then send unique signals (specific to each color) towards the brain so we can distinguish different colors. Sunlight is composed of visible colors, which are known as colors of the rainbow (Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet). This combination is known as white light. When white light strikes an white object, we see white. That is because the white objects reflects all colors equally and doesn't absorb any light. On the contrary, black objects absorb all the light equally and doesn't reflect any white light. For different colours, some might absorb some wavelengths but reflect others.

Different colours have different size wavelengths. The range of wavelength varies from 400 nanometers (Violet) to 700 nanometers (Red). Shorter wavelengths scatter more than longer waves.

  • Red : 620-750nm
  • Orange : 590-620nm
  • Yellow : 570-590nm
  • Green : 495-570nm
  • Blue : 450-495nm
  • Indigo : 420-440nm
  • Violet : 380-450nm

With this information, we can understand a question which you might've not thought before; Why is the sky blue? The sky is blue due to a phenomenon known as Reyleigh scattering. The white light interacts with air molecules, causing blue light to scatter more than other colors. But you might wonder why the sky isn't violet? Even though it's true that violet scatters even more than blue, our eyes are more sensitive to blue light and the sun emits more blue light than violet light.
